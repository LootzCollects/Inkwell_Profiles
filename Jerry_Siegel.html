JERRY SIEGEL

Writer and Co-Creator of Superman

Key Facts

  • Born: October 17, 1914 (Cleveland, U.S.)
  • Field: Writer, Innovator of the Superhero Genre
  • Debut: 1938 (*Action Comics* #1)
  • Notable Creations (w/ Joe Shuster):
    • Superman
    • Lois Lane & Clark Kent
    • The Spectre
    • Superboy

Biography & Influence

Jerry Siegel, alongside artist Joe Shuster, fundamentally changed the landscape of popular culture by co-creating **Superman**, establishing the superhero archetype that dominates entertainment to this day. Working out of Cleveland, Ohio, during the Great Depression, Siegel and Shuster spent years pitching their creation, which evolved from a bald, telepathic villain into the iconic champion of the oppressed. Superman finally debuted in *Action Comics* #1 (1938), instantaneously launching the Golden Age of Comics.

Siegel’s writing established core elements of the Superman mythos, including the concept of Clark Kent's mild-mannered secret identity, the supporting cast of Lois Lane and Lex Luthor, and the origin story of Krypton. Beyond Superman, Siegel also co-created the supernatural hero **The Spectre** for DC. Despite creating what would become a multi-billion dollar enterprise, Siegel and Shuster famously sold the rights to Superman for a mere $130, leading to a decades-long legal battle for creative recognition and financial compensation—a struggle that defined the early fight for creator rights in the comic book industry.

On the Creation of Superman

In this rare 1981 BBC interview, Jerry Siegel and Joe Shuster reflect on their creative partnership that began in their high school years in Cleveland, Ohio, and their exhaustive, long-running struggle to get the concept of Superman published. They discuss the initial spark of the idea—Superman starting as a telepathic villain before being reimagined as the iconic champion of the oppressed—and the moment they finally sold the rights to Detective Comics, Inc. The conversation is framed by the poignant reality of their long and famous legal battles for creator credit and compensation, highlighting the bittersweet nature of having created one of the world's most enduring cultural icons.
