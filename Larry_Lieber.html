LARRY LIEBER

Comic Book Professional

Key Facts

  • Born: c. 1930 (Younger brother of Stan Lee)
  • Field: Writer, Artist, Co-Creator, Scripter
  • Debut: 1951 (All True Crime #44)
  • Notable Works:
    • Co-Creator of Thor, Iron Man, and Ant-Man
    • Co-Created the name Uru (for Mjolnir)
    • Longest-running artist on The Amazing Spider-Man newspaper strip

Biography & Influence

Larry Lieber is an American comic book writer and artist whose foundational contributions during the Silver Age helped establish the modern Marvel Universe. As the younger brother of Stan Lee, he began working as an art assistant in the late 1940s and served in the US Air Force before dedicating himself to comics full-time in the 1950s.

He is perhaps best known for his critical scripting and creative work alongside Jack Kirby on some of Marvel’s most iconic heroes. Lieber co-created both Thor (first appearance in Journey Into Mystery #83) and Iron Man (first appearance in Tales of Suspense #39), giving them their civilian identities as Donald Blake and Anthony Stark. He also co-created Ant-Man (Hank Pym). Furthermore, he inadvertently created the magical metal Uru (used for Mjolnir) simply by devising a short, exotic-sounding word the letterer could easily fit on the page.

Lieber’s career also saw a long run both writing and drawing the Western comic Rawhide Kid, and he holds the record as the longest-running contributor, both writing and drawing, the syndicated The Amazing Spider-Man newspaper comic strip from 1986 until his retirement from penciling in 2018. In his later career, he achieved a lifelong goal of writing original prose with the publication of his novel, Chirps.

Interview & Commentary

In this highly personal interview, Larry Lieber reflects on his career, including how his perceived slowness led his brother Stan Lee to offer him the freedom to work on Rawhide Kid. He shares the charming story behind creating the term "Uru" and the personal motivation for writing his novel, Chirps, which helped him process grief after his wife's passing. Lieber discusses the creative freedom of writing his own prose compared to the collaborative nature of comics.
